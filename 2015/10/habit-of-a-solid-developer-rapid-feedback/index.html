You've likely heard of the idea of Failing Fast. In the end, failing fast is nothing more but a type of feedback.

Feedback cycles can be applied to many aspects software development and life in general.

Try pair programming, having a partner watch for and point out silly mistakes, or propose alternative approaches. The instant feedback is hugely beneficial. Leverage the feedback of other tools such as a compiler, unit tests, or manual QA.

Don't make assumptions about how something works, even though the principle of least surprise is nice to follow, don't assume that's how it works. Prove it, test it, fail fast or gather feedback on the topic.

Test all assumptions and prove to yourself that the assumptions are either right or wrong. This could come down to how you expect a library function to behave for certain inputs, or this could apply to how you think the customer wanted you to implement a feature.

Don't wait till the product is shipped to learn that's not what your customer asked for, give them a call, or email, or instant message them...

Happy Feedback! <-- wait what?
